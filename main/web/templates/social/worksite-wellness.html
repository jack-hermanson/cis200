Worksite Wellness with Dr. Bob Lefavi

Poor health and well-being have negative effects on work productivity. It makes sense for businesses to promote employee health, as this would benefit the entire business, from the employees themselves to the customers. In a study about nurses' physical and mental health, it was found that over half of the nurses reported suboptimal health. Compared to the nurses who reported better health, nurses reporting poorer health also had higher rates of medical errors. The health and well-being of employees in the workplace have real impacts and should be a high priority for all organizations.

However, sometimes it's the workplace that is the source of health problems. Bullying, low social support at work, organizational injustice, and effort-reward imbalance are just a few of many job stressors that influence employee health. Exposure to these job stressors predicts elevated risks for poor health behaviors as well as chronic illnesses like cardiovascular disease. Reducing and preventing as much exposure to job stressors as well as improving the psychosocial quality of work (relating to the interrelation of social factors and individual thought and behavior) could prevent a substantial amount of common mental health problems. Offering the necessities like health care and employee assistance programs to help those who may be afraid to reach out to their employer or any peer is a great start to improving worksite mental health.

One major issue is that a vast majority of workers struggling with mental health and depression do not use mental health services. Physical health is highly correlated with mental health, and a recent study suggests that physical activity has a strong impact on improving mental health. Despite this, only about 25 percent of the U.S. adult population meets the federal guidelines for physical activity. The percent is lower for individuals who are minorities or from lower income households. This leaves organizations trying to manage a large pool of unhealthy employees. Worksite wellness programs have the potential to improve employee health, medical costs, and presenteeism, but so far, the evidence is not clear that worksite wellness programs are worth the investment.

To better understand the historical trends and future outlook of worksite wellness programs, our team interviewed a worksite wellness expert, Dr. Bob Lefavi, who has been in the field of health promotion since the 1980s. Dr. Lefavi is a professor and founding dean of the University of South Carolina's Beaufort campus. His first job was the development of a hospital-based worksite wellness program, and he continued to consult for other businesses, such as Delta Air Lines, interested in wellness and health promotion programs. In the interview with Dr. Lefavi, our team learned that the challenges with worksite wellness are multifold, to include the importance of top management and leadership to show support and vulnerability to employees to remove the stigma from having health issues. Overall, there are many challenges to overcome to achieve worksite wellness, and we can start by improving our understanding of the issues. We hope you learn as much from the interview with Dr. Lefavi as we did.
