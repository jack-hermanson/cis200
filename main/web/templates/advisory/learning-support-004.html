Learning Support

Elizabeth Pickrel:

Throughout the semester, I have learned that people and processes are the main reason issues occur in information systems and in the workplace. While working on creating the final exam, communication issues occurred due to the two learning support teams being in different classes. The lack of communication between the teams, especially when large changes were made during class, resulted in many people being confused and frustrated with each other. The most important thing I learned is that I had to actively work to communicate with the other team to let them know what we were working on and what changes had been made to keep everyone on the same page and keep the work running smoothly. This issue existed within our team as well. Some people were able to stay after class or attend the Friday meetings and would speak with the professor separately. This created an issue when we did not share all the information discussed with the professor to our other team members, leaving members confused about our tasks and what parts of our plan had changed. Breaking down these information silos by finding new ways to communicate (such as group texts or chatting on Microsoft Teams) was required for our team to be able to effectively work with our team members and with other teams as well. I also learned that intrinsic motivation and being a self-starter are the best ways to work effectively in a team, as they allow for me to look for tasks that I see need to be done and to begin working on those tasks or delegating them to others. This is how gaps in work are filled and helps group work move more smoothly. Lastly, I learned that I am a capable leader and am skilled in helping others, but that I need to work on asking for help and letting others know when I have too much on my plate. I am just one person and asking for help from others allows for different perspectives and creates better work overall.
