Livestock Sustainability with Dr. Temple Grandin

Livestock sustainability is a moral issue, with the management of livestock having impacts on ethical animal welfare and the global climate. Potential solutions include fighting for better policies, producing fewer meat products from livestock, and being a responsible consumer, among other solutions.

To learn more about these challenges and potential solutions, two teams conducted research on these topics. Each team also had the privilege of interviewing Dr. Temple Grandin, a world-renowned livestock sustainability expert at Colorado State University.

What we learned from the interviews is that livestock sustainability is a complicated issue, with many layers and stakeholders involved. While it can be easy to find information about how livestock grazing damages the environment, it's not as well known that using grazing animals in crop rotation can improve the land in some cases. Dr. Grandin learned from an agronomist who visited CSU that the very best crop land in the United States, in Iowa and Illinois, was created by herds of grazing bison. There are huge amounts of land in the world that could be improved with specialized grazing and crop rotation.

As Dr. Grandin says, "we must give animals a life worth living." She talked about how livestock are raised and managed with the need for more humane practices, despite industry pushback. Along these lines, Dr. Grandin has observed for decades in her consulting work that poor management is often the root cause of many of the problems in the livestock industry. Dr. Grandin made it very clear that we "need to get the suits out of the office." She talked about managers who never step foot in any of the plants they manage and, instead, spend a lot of time behind spreadsheets, where "everything is an abstraction." She was very passionate about how management needs to spend more time "in the world of the practical" and see first-hand the people and processes they are managing. In fact, she was very clear that she would much prefer good management with outdated but adequate equipment at a plant rather than poor management with the latest and greatest equipment.

Because the supply chains in the livestock industry are big and complex, Dr. Grandin emphasized that "big is fragile," meaning that these large, complicated systems are easy to break. A business solution offered by Dr. Grandin would be to have more distributed supply chains, like the beer industry has. She talked about how we have a gigantic Budweiser plant locally with many small micro-breweries to serve mixed markets, suggesting that we could try to set up these types of distributed supply chains for livestock sustainability.

Overall, our teams learned a great deal from Dr. Temple Grandin, and we would like to thank her for taking time to meet with each team separately. As one of our team members reflected, "my interview with Dr. Temple Grandin was one of the most special and eye-opening things I did in this class. She taught us the value of seeing things as practically as possible and not as an abstraction. She said, 'we have to make them [CEOs and corporate bigwigs] see that they are not just figures on a spreadsheet.' You can also take this to mean that the more practically you see things, the more influence that you can have on their systems as you know their inner workings." It's important for business students and professionals to understand the inner workings of the systems we're embedded in if we are going to achieve business sustainability across the globe.
