Wells Fargo Cross-Selling Scandal

Wells Fargo is a financial services company that focuses on customer relationships and keeping an actively engaged sales culture. In 2013, it was revealed that Wells Fargo employees had been engaged in a cross-selling scandal. Prior to the scandal, Wells Fargo had a great reputation and was even named "the big bank least tarnished by the scandals and reputational crises" by American Banker.

Cross-selling is about selling the most products from Wells Fargo to customers. The more products their customers have, the more information the bank has on their customers, which helps them make better banking decisions about credit, products, and pricing.

The cross-selling scandal involved Wells Fargo employees using aggressive selling tactics to meet their quotas. Employees were fired for opening accounts with debit or credit cards without the customers' knowledge. Some said Wells Fargo's daily sales target put an excessive amount of pressure on employees to meet that target, leading to these sales integrity violations.

Wells Fargo had policies in place to prevent employees from ignoring sales integrity. These policies included the Executive Officer Stock Ownership Policy, Unearned Compensation Recoupment, Extended Clawback Policy, and Performance-Based Vesting Conditions. However, these were not sufficient to prevent this scandal.

In the end, Wells Fargo announced it would pay $185 million to settle a lawsuit filed by regulators and the city and county of Los Angeles, and the company admitted to employees opening up to two million accounts without the customers' knowledge. The bank announced actions that would be taken to improve its internal controls. This included hiring an independent consulting firm, terminating over 5000 employees, eliminating product goals, and more.
