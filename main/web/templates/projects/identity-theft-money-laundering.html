Online Identity Theft and Money Laundering

Online identity theft can lead to your banking accounts being used for money laundering. Our team interviewed Mr. Doug Burian, a U.S. Marine Corps Major who lost thousands of dollars as a victim to this cybercrime. Because this can happen to anyone with a checking account, our team opted to present Mr. Burian's interview as a news story, as we believe his story needs to be heard.

Many financial information systems do not automatically flag electronic funds tranfer amounts under $5,000, so cybercriminals will transfer amounts around or right under $5,000 to avoid these alerts. You should check your daily transfer limits at your bank to determine how many times a cybercriminal could set up electronic funds transfers of around $5,000 from your banking accounts without hitting your bank's daily limit.

Mr. Doug Burian engaged in extensive information gathering to learn what happened to his money. Through his research, he discovered the unauthorized transfers were for the purposes of money laundering and that the out-of-state account where his money was transferred belonged to banking customers who were also likely defrauded from the cybercrime. To learn more about Mr. Burian's story, you can read 'Am I in the Twilight Zone?' Marine has to eat the loss after $10,000 is looted from his USAA Bank account, published in the San Antonio Express-News in 2020.

We thank Mr. Burian for sharing his story, which we can all learn from to engage in more secure banking. Our team is also grateful to CSU's Collegian TV (CTV) for allowing us to use their news room studio for our recording.

Anti Money Laundering Laws
  • Bank Secrecy Act (1970)
  • Money Laundering Control Act (1986)
  • Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1988
  • Annunzio-Wylie Anti-Money Laundering Act (1992)
  • Money Laundering Suppression Act (1994)
  • Money Laundering and Financial Crimes Strategy Act (1998)
  • Uniting and Strengthening America by Providing Appropriate Tools Required to Intercept and Obstruct Terrorism Act of 2001 (USA PATRIOT Act)
  • Intelligence Reform & Terrorism Prevention Act (2004)
Ways to Change Your Security to Avoid this Happening to You
  • Use antivirus software, firewall, strong passwords, multi-factor authentication
  • Make sure your routers are up to date (research new routers every few years to ensure that your technology is current)
  • Do not click on or follow suspicious links/attachments
  • Use answers to security questions that nobody could find about you online
  • Manually and frequently check your bank accounts
  • Learn about all services you're automatically opted into with online and mobile banking, and work with your bank to disable/opt out of any services you do not use
  • Think like a criminal and then mitigate risks accordingly
