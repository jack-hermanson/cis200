Project Management

The PM team created a video to explain their role and lessons learned in the website project. They also researched leadership styles and the Scrum framework, described in the team’s summary below.

Leadership is not so simple to define, as it comes in many shapes and forms. Through other definitions and research, we developed our own definition that perfectly fits what we are trying to achieve in our overall project. Our new definition of leadership for this project is “being in a position to lead others in a group or organization towards a common goal in bettering the business world.” Using this definition as a guideline, we can move towards changing the business world for the better in many ways. Throughout our project, the whole class dealt with many forms of adversity and had to overcome them. By delegating tasks, workflows, and goals throughout each group, members had the opportunity to make their own contributions in researching their respective business for a better world topics.

To inform our work as project managers, we researched leadership styles and explored styles that mapped to our strengths and weaknesses as leaders, so we could identify areas we needed to develop and improve while honing and teaching peers our strengths.

Five common leadership styles are:

  • Authoritarian (Autocratic) Leadership,
  • Participative (Democratic) Leadership,
  • Delegative (Laissez-Faire) Leadership,
  • Transactional Leadership, and
  • Transformational Leadership.

Each of these has their own respective pros and cons and different situations where one would work better than the rest. Throughout the project, we were charged with reflecting on our leadership styles and where we needed to improve.

Another key insight that emerged through our project is that leadership through managers is only one of the required ingredients for project success. Leaders cannot exist without followers. One of the project managers, Ian Klava, communicated the importance of followership as a core facilitator of productivity and commitment in the project. Followership integrates the traits, behaviors, and outcomes of followers in the leadership process, offering a more holistic view than leader-centric views alone. For example, whether project members were proactive, passive, supportive, or resistant made an impact on the overall project.

The Scrum framework was mostly implemented by the IS team, trained by TAs Jack Hermanson and Mahlet Amde. Other teams struggled to put Scrum to practice, even though most teams used a Scrum board to some degree to manage their projects.

The values of Scrum are commitment, focus, openness, respect, and courage, which should promote effective followership in the leadership process. Future teams should make a real effort to implement the Scrum framework and remain committed to its core values throughout your time working on the website project.
